Performed a regression analysis project in a Statistics & Probability course using Microsoft Excel to evaluate 
relationships between behavioral outcomes and demographic, academic, and workload-related variables. 
Analyzed data involving STEM major status, workload outside of school,
race, household size, and academic unit load.

Applied hypothesis testing and p-value analysis to identify statistically significant relationships within the dataset. 
Findings indicated that external workload demonstrated the strongest correlation with shower frequency, 
while variables such as academic major and race showed limited statistical significance. 
Presented conclusions through organized data interpretation and quantitative analysis.

Data for this study was collected through a convenience sample of 33 students surveyed around campus. 
The dependent variable measured was the number of showers taken per week. Independent variables included race, 
academic major, household size, religion, number of enrolled school units, and overall workload outside of school. 
The purpose of the study was to examine whether demographic, academic, and workload-related factors influenced 
personal hygiene habits.

The initial hypothesis proposed that students enrolled in traditionally rigorous majors, particularly STEM fields, 
would report fewer showers per week due to increased academic demands and workload. This hypothesis served as the 
null hypothesis for the analysis.

To evaluate the relationships between variables, regression analysis was conducted using Microsoft Excel. 
Multiple regression models were tested, and statistical significance was evaluated primarily through p-value analysis. 
The results indicated that workload outside of school demonstrated the strongest statistically significant relationship 
with shower frequency (p < 0.05). Variables such as STEM major status and race also showed some correlation within the 
dataset, although workload remained the most influential factor overall.

The analysis suggested that individuals with higher overall workloads tended to report taking more showers, 
regardless of race or academic major. Based on these findings, the original hypothesis that STEM majors would 
exhibit lower levels of personal hygiene was not supported by the data, leading to the rejection of the null hypothesis.
